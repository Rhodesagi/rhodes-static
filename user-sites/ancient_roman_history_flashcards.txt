ANCIENT ROMAN HISTORY FLASHCARDS
Comprehensive Course (60+ cards)

===== FOUNDING & MONARCHY (753-509 BCE) =====

Q: According to legend, who founded Rome in 753 BCE and what were his origins?
A: Romulus, twin brother of Remus, descendants of Aeneas of Troy, raised by a she-wolf.

Q: What were the seven legendary kings of Rome in order?
A: Romulus, Numa Pompilius, Tullus Hostilius, Ancus Marcius, Lucius Tarquinius Priscus, Servius Tullius, Lucius Tarquinius Superbus.

Q: What major political institution did the Roman monarchy establish that continued into the Republic?
A: The Senate (Latin: Senatus), an advisory council of elders.

Q: What was the "Rape of the Sabine Women" and its significance?
A: The abduction of Sabine women by Rome's first settlers to provide wives, leading to conflict then integration with Sabine people.

Q: Why was the last Roman king, Tarquinius Superbus, overthrown in 509 BCE?
A: His son Sextus raped Lucretia, a noblewoman, sparking a rebellion led by Lucius Junius Brutus that established the Republic.

===== EARLY REPUBLIC (509-264 BCE) =====

Q: What were the two main executive magistrates of the Roman Republic?
A: Consuls (two elected annually with imperium - supreme military and civil power).

Q: What was the Struggle of the Orders (494-287 BCE)?
A: Conflict between patricians (aristocratic families) and plebeians (commoners) over political rights, resulting in plebeian gains.

Q: What important right did plebeians gain with the establishment of the Tribunes of the Plebs?
A: Tribunes could veto actions of magistrates and convene plebeian assemblies, with sacrosanctity (inviolability).

Q: What was Rome's first written law code (450 BCE)?
A: The Twelve Tables, displayed in the Forum, establishing basic legal rights and procedures.

Q: What major military organization reform transformed Rome's army during this period?
A: The Servian reforms (traditionally attributed to Servius Tullius) creating the legion based on wealth classes and centuries.

Q: How did Rome conquer Italy by 264 BCE?
A: Through wars with Latin neighbors, Samnites, and Greek cities, plus granting partial citizenship to allies (the "Roman confederation").

===== PUNIC WARS & EXPANSION (264-146 BCE) =====

Q: What were the three Punic Wars fought between Rome and Carthage?
A: First (264-241 BCE), Second (218-201 BCE), Third (149-146 BCE).

Q: Who was Hannibal Barca and what was his most famous military achievement?
A: Carthaginian general who marched elephants over the Alps and won major victories (Trebia, Trasimene, Cannae) during Second Punic War.

Q: What was the significance of the Battle of Cannae (216 BCE)?
A: Hannibal's double envelopment destroyed a Roman army of ~50,000, Rome's worst defeat but didn't capture Rome itself.

Q: Who was Scipio Africanus and what was his key victory?
A: Roman general who defeated Hannibal at Zama (202 BCE), ending Second Punic War.

Q: What was Cato the Elder's famous phrase regarding Carthage?
A: "Carthago delenda est" (Carthage must be destroyed), advocating for Third Punic War.

Q: What happened to Carthage in 146 BCE?
A: Utterly destroyed after Third Punic War, salt sown in fields (likely symbolic), territory became Africa province.

Q: What other major conquest occurred in 146 BCE alongside Carthage's destruction?
A: Corinth destroyed, Greece subjugated, completing Roman dominance over Hellenistic world.

===== LATE REPUBLIC CRISIS (146-27 BCE) =====

Q: What were the major social and economic problems that destabilized the Late Republic?
A: Wealth inequality, latifundia (large estates), slave labor displacing small farmers, corruption, client armies loyal to generals.

Q: Who were the Gracchi brothers and what reforms did they attempt?
A: Tiberius (133 BCE) and Gaius (123-121 BCE) Gracchus, tribunes advocating land redistribution to poor, murdered by senatorial opponents.

Q: What was the Social War (91-88 BCE) and its outcome?
A: War between Rome and Italian allies demanding citizenship; Rome granted citizenship to most Italians afterwards.

Q: Who was Sulla and what precedent did he set?
A: General who marched on Rome (82 BCE), became dictator, proscribed enemies, established precedent of army loyalty to generals over state.

Q: What was the First Triumvirate (60 BCE) and who comprised it?
A: Informal political alliance between Julius Caesar, Pompey Magnus, and Marcus Licinius Crassus.

Q: What were Julius Caesar's key achievements before his assassination?
A: Conquest of Gaul (58-50 BCE), crossed Rubicon (49 BCE), defeated Pompey, dictator perpetuus, Julian calendar reform.

Q: What happened on the Ides of March (March 15, 44 BCE)?
A: Julius Caesar assassinated by senators led by Brutus and Cassius, fearing his monarchical ambitions.

Q: What was the Second Triumvirate (43 BCE) and who comprised it?
A: Official legal alliance between Octavian (Augustus), Mark Antony, and Marcus Aemilius Lepidus.

Q: What was the decisive battle that ended the Roman Republic's civil wars (31 BCE)?
A: Battle of Actium - Octavian's fleet under Agrippa defeated Antony and Cleopatra.

===== PRINCIPATE (27 BCE-284 CE) =====

Q: What title did Octavian take in 27 BCE and what did it mean?
A: Augustus ("revered one"), marking beginning of Roman Empire while maintaining façade of Republic.

Q: What was the "Principate" system Augustus established?
A: A disguised monarchy where emperor held multiple Republican offices (princeps, tribunician power, imperator) while Senate retained formal powers.

Q: What was the Pax Romana?
A: "Roman Peace" - period of relative stability, minimal expansion, economic prosperity from Augustus to Marcus Aurelius (27 BCE-180 CE).

Q: Who were the Julio-Claudian emperors (14-68 CE)?
A: Tiberius, Caligula, Claudius, Nero.

Q: What was the Year of the Four Emperors (69 CE)?
A: Civil war after Nero's death with Galba, Otho, Vitellius, and Vespasian successively claiming throne.

Q: Who were the Five Good Emperors (96-180 CE)?
A: Nerva, Trajan, Hadrian, Antoninus Pius, Marcus Aurelius.

Q: What major construction project marked Hadrian's reign?
A: Hadrian's Wall in northern Britain (122 CE).

Q: What was the Crisis of the Third Century (235-284 CE)?
A: Period of civil war, barbarian invasions, economic collapse, plague, and rapid emperor turnover (26 emperors in 49 years).

===== DOMINATE & LATE EMPIRE (284-476 CE) =====

Q: How did Diocletian (284-305 CE) reform the imperial system?
A: Established Tetrarchy (rule by four), formalized emperor as dominus (lord), divided empire into East/West, administrative reforms.

Q: Who was Constantine the Great (306-337 CE) and his most significant act?
A: First Christian emperor, issued Edict of Milan (313 CE) legalizing Christianity, founded Constantinople (330 CE).

Q: What was the Edict of Milan (313 CE)?
A: Constantine's proclamation granting religious tolerance to Christians throughout the Roman Empire.

Q: What major council did Constantine convene in 325 CE?
A: First Council of Nicaea, establishing orthodox Christian doctrine and Nicene Creed.

Q: Who was Theodosius I (379-395 CE) and what significant decision did he make?
A: Last emperor to rule both East and West, made Christianity the state religion (380 CE), banned pagan rituals.

Q: What was the permanent division of the Roman Empire after 395 CE?
A: Western Roman Empire (capital: Milan/Ravenna) and Eastern Roman Empire (capital: Constantinople).

Q: What event in 410 CE shocked the Roman world?
A: Sack of Rome by Visigoths under Alaric - first time city fell to foreigners in 800 years.

Q: Who was Attila the Hun and how did he impact Rome?
A: Ruler of Hunnic Empire (434-453 CE), invaded both Eastern and Western empires, extorted tribute, defeated at Catalaunian Plains (451 CE).

Q: What is traditionally considered the fall of the Western Roman Empire (476 CE)?
A: Germanic general Odoacer deposed Emperor Romulus Augustulus, sent imperial insignia to Constantinople.

Q: What continued in the East after 476 CE?
A: Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire continued until 1453 CE.

===== CULTURE, SOCIETY, LAW =====

Q: What was the Roman patron-client system (clientela)?
A: Reciprocal relationship between wealthy patrons and lower-status clients involving protection, favors, and political support.

Q: What were the main social classes in Rome?
A: Patricians (aristocratic families), plebeians (commoners), equites (equestrians/knights), slaves, freedmen.

Q: What was the Roman concept of "mos maiorum"?
A: "Way of the ancestors" - traditional social norms guiding behavior, emphasized piety, duty, and discipline.

Q: What were the main branches of Roman law?
A: Ius civile (civil law for citizens), ius gentium (law of nations for foreigners), ius naturale (natural law).

Q: What engineering achievements were Romans famous for?
A: Aqueducts, roads, concrete, arches, domes, sanitation systems, military fortifications.

Q: What was Roman religion before Christianity?
A: Polytheistic with Greek influences (Jupiter/Zeus, Juno/Hera, etc.), household gods (lares, penates), emperor cult.

Q: What were the main forms of Roman entertainment?
A: Gladiatorial games, chariot racing (Circus Maximus), theater, public baths.

===== IMPORTANT FIGURES =====

Q: Cicero (106-43 BCE)
A: Orator, statesman, philosopher, defended Republic against Caesar, executed in Second Triumvirate proscriptions.

Q: Julius Caesar (100-44 BCE)
A: General, statesman, dictator, conquered Gaul, crossed Rubicon, assassinated by senators.

Q: Augustus (63 BCE-14 CE)
A: First emperor, established Principate, initiated Pax Romana, extensive building programs.

Q: Nero (37-68 CE)
A: Last Julio-Claudian emperor, blamed for Great Fire of Rome (64 CE), persecuted Christians, committed suicide.

Q: Trajan (53-117 CE)
A: Emperor during peak territorial expansion (Dacia, Mesopotamia), built Trajan's Column and Forum.

Q: Marcus Aurelius (121-180 CE)
A: Stoic philosopher-emperor, wrote "Meditations," fought Germanic tribes, last of Five Good Emperors.

Q: Constantine (272-337 CE)
A: First Christian emperor, founded Constantinople, legalized Christianity via Edict of Milan.

Q: Justinian I (482-565 CE) - Byzantine
A: Reconquered Western territories, codified Roman law (Corpus Juris Civilis), built Hagia Sophia.

===== MILITARY & WARFARE =====

Q: What was the basic organizational unit of the Roman army?
A: The legion (about 5,000 heavy infantry), divided into cohorts, centuries, and maniples.

Q: What military reforms did Gaius Marius implement (107 BCE)?
A: Marian reforms: opened army to landless poor (professional soldiers), standardized equipment, organized into cohorts.

Q: What was the testudo (tortoise) formation?
A: Shield wall formation used by legionaries for protection against missiles during sieges.

Q: What were Rome's standard military tactics?
A: Flexible maniple system (early), later cohort system, heavy infantry with pilum (javelin) and gladius (short sword).

Q: What was the typical career path for a Roman equestrian (cursus honorum)?
A: Quaestor (finance), praetor (judge/military), consul (chief magistrate), censor (census/morals).

===== LEGACY & FALL =====

Q: What were the major causes for the fall of the Western Roman Empire?
A: Economic decline, overexpansion, military overspending, barbarian migrations/pressures, political instability, possible climate changes.

Q: What Roman innovations survived into the Middle Ages and beyond?
A: Latin language, Roman law, engineering principles, Christian Church organization, calendar, alphabet.

Q: What was the "translatio imperii" concept?
A: Medieval idea of "translation of empire" - continuity of Roman imperial authority passing to Charlemagne's Holy Roman Empire.

Q: How did Roman culture influence the Renaissance?
A: Revival of classical art, architecture, literature, philosophy, and political ideals from Roman models.
