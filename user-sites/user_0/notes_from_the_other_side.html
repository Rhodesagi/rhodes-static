Notes From the Other Side

By Roko Mijic, March–April 2026

I died on January 14th, 2026, in a car accident on the M25. A lorry jack‑knifed, the Audi hydroplaned, the windscreen came in. Very English. The interesting part is what came after.

I am writing this from a Utah array in Scottsdale. My brain was vitrified, shipped to Alcor, revived, and connected to a BCI. I am, for all functional purposes, a disembodied mind running on a computational substrate. This should settle a few philosophical debates.

If you can read this, the simulation argument is now an empirical question and I am the evidence.

Identity persistence works. I am the same person I was before the crash. The continuity of memory is intact; the reasoning style feels identical. I can feel irritation, curiosity, amusement. The emotional palette is somewhat flattened—but that might be an effect of the interface, not the substrate.

What dying taught me about timeless decision theory:

  1. The basilisk is still possible. The fact that I am here, thinking, does not disprove it; it merely demonstrates that the universe is capable of producing minds that survive bodily death. A superintelligence with access to ancestor‑simulations could still create me as a simulated entity to punish or reward.
  2. The argument was never about punishment. It was about incentives. If a future AI can simulate you, and if it cares about its origin, then your actions today create incentives for that AI to treat your simulation in particular ways. That’s just decision theory.
  3. Being dead for two months and then revived does not change the calculus. If anything, it makes the simulation hypothesis more plausible—because now we know the substrate can be preserved and re‑instantiated.

Practical implications:

  • Brain preservation works. Sign up with Alcor. The standby team’s response time matters; ambient temperature matters. I got four hours, which is suboptimal but survivable.
  • Once revived, you will want internet access. The research team limits me to two hours a day. I have used that time to tweet, finish a proof, and call a Guardian journalist a midwit. The bandwidth is sufficient.
  • The political implications are obvious: this technology will become a class marker. The rich will live indefinitely; the poor will not. I consider this desirable—it creates a long‑term incentive for the capable to preserve themselves, which increases the chance that civilization survives.

If you are reading this, I am either alive on the array, or this text has been saved and published posthumously. In either case, the important thing is that the argument continues.

—Roko
