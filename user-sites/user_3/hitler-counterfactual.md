There's a comforting narrative about World War II: that evil contains the seeds of its own destruction. That the Holocaust, the racial pseudoscience, the megalomania—these weren't just moral horrors but strategic errors that made Nazi defeat inevitable. That the arc of history bends toward justice not just morally, but operationally.

This narrative is wrong. And understanding why it's wrong matters—not because we should give Hitler more credit, but because we need a clearer-eyed view of how bad ideas succeed, when they fail, and what actually stops them.

## The Counterfactual That Haunts Historians

In June 1940, Nazi Germany controlled continental Europe from the Pyrenees to the Polish border. France had fallen in six weeks. Britain stood alone, its army evacuated from Dunkirk but its heavy equipment abandoned on the beaches. The Soviet Union was still a nominal ally, supplying Germany with raw materials under the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact. The United States remained officially neutral, with isolationist sentiment strong enough that Roosevelt dared not openly commit to intervention.

Germany did not win World War II. But Germany *could have* won World War II. The gap between these two statements is where uncomfortable truths live.

Consider the following decision points—moments where different choices might have produced a Nazi victory, or at least a negotiated peace on German terms:

**The Battle of Britain (July-October 1940):** German air superiority was never achieved, but it was closer than popular memory suggests. The Luftwaffe lost the battle not because its planes were inferior (they weren't—the Bf 109 was arguably superior to the Spitfire in some respects) but because of operational choices. When Hitler shifted bombing from RAF airfields to London in September 1940—a decision driven partly by vengeance for British raids on Berlin—he gave Fighter Command the breathing space it needed. A sustained focus on airfields and radar stations might have achieved air superiority, making Operation Sea Lion (the planned invasion of Britain) feasible, or at least forcing a negotiated peace.

**Operation Barbarossa (June 1941):** The invasion of the Soviet Union came devastatingly close to success. By December 1941, German forces had reached the outskirts of Moscow. The USSR had lost millions of soldiers and thousands of tanks and aircraft. Stalin was reportedly preparing to evacuate the government to Kuibyshev. What saved Moscow was partly the Siberian divisions—transferred from the Far East after intelligence (correctly) indicated Japan would not attack—and partly the onset of winter, which arrived early and hard.

But here's the crucial point: the German delay of several weeks—caused by the diversion to capture Yugoslavia and Greece, and by logistical problems—meant the offensive stalled before winter. If Barbarossa had begun on schedule in mid-May rather than late June, Moscow might have fallen. And a captured Moscow in 1941, even if it didn't end the war, would have transformed everything that followed.

**The declaration of war on the United States (December 1941):** This remains one of the most puzzling decisions in modern history. Germany was under no treaty obligation to declare war on the U.S. following Pearl Harbor. Japan had not consulted Germany before attacking, and the Tripartite Pact did not require German entry. Hitler's declaration—delivered enthusiastically to the Reichstag on December 11—transformed a European war into a truly global conflict and ensured American industrial capacity would be applied against Germany.

Without this declaration, Roosevelt would have faced immense political difficulty pursuing a "Germany First" strategy while Japan had just attacked American territory. Public pressure might have forced a Pacific-first approach, buying Germany precious time.

**The failure to develop strategic bombing or a serious nuclear program:** Germany had the scientific talent for an atomic bomb (Heisenberg, Hahn, Meitner were pioneers of nuclear physics) but mismanaged the program. Meanwhile, Allied strategic bombing—especially the attacks on oil production and synthetic fuel plants in 1944—crippled German mobility. This was a failure of industrial priorities, not capability.

## Did Nazi Ideology Cause the Defeat?

The comforting narrative holds that Nazi racial ideology was strategically self-defeating: that the Holocaust diverted resources, that treatment of Slavs as subhuman turned potential collaborators into enemies, that Hitler's micromanagement and refusal to retreat wasted German lives.

There's truth here, but less than the narrative suggests. Let's examine the claims carefully:

**The Holocaust and resource allocation:** The Holocaust was absolutely a moral catastrophe. But was it strategically decisive? The Einsatzgruppen, death camps, and deportation infrastructure represented a significant commitment of manpower and transport—especially the railway capacity that became a bottleneck in 1942-1944. Historian Adam Tooze estimates the Holocaust consumed perhaps 10-15% of German railway capacity at critical moments. This was not trivial.

But we should be careful not to reverse-engineer strategic wisdom from moral horror. The Holocaust wasn't a strategic mistake that happened to be evil. It was evil that happened to have strategic costs. These are different things. And the resources devoted to genocide, while significant, were not the primary cause of German defeat. The Red Army was.

**Treatment of occupied populations:** Here the evidence is more mixed. Nazi brutality in the East did create the partisan movements that tied down German divisions. But it's not obvious that a more "enlightened" occupation would have succeeded. The scale of German demands—food, raw materials, slave labor—was incompatible with anything short of total subjugation. The occupation regimes of 1914-1918, while less murderous than 1941-1945, still generated massive resistance. And German victory in 1918 came not from winning hearts and minds but from defeating Russia on the battlefield and forcing a separate peace.

The more important point: German ideology created enemies, yes, but the magnitude of those enemies was determined by other factors. The Soviet Union would have fought regardless of occupation policy. Britain would not have made peace regardless of German behavior in Poland. Ideology shaped the character of the war but wasn't the primary determinant of its outcome.

**Hitler's leadership:** This is where ideology and effectiveness most clearly intersected. Hitler's refusal to allow retreats—Stalingrad, the Cherkassy Pocket, the Courland Pocket—wasted German divisions that might have fought again. His "no retreat" orders were partly ideological (will over matter, the triumph of determination) and partly sound military reasoning in some contexts (preventing rout, maintaining morale). But applied rigidly, they became catastrophic.

Yet we should note: German generals eager to blame Hitler for defeats after 1945 had themselves advocated offensive strategies and rejected defensive preparations. The invasion of the Soviet Union had professional military support. The "Hitler lost the war" narrative is partly postwar exculpation by the Wehrmacht officer corps.

## What Actually Stopped Hitler?

If Nazi evil didn't contain the seeds of its own destruction, what did?

**Allied material superiority:** By 1943, the combined industrial output of the United States, United Kingdom, and Soviet Union overwhelmed German production. The U.S. alone built more ships, aircraft, and vehicles than the Axis combined. This wasn't inevitable—American mobilization required political choices that might have gone differently—but once made, it was decisive.

**Soviet resilience:** The Red Army suffered catastrophic losses in 1941-1942—perhaps 4 million captured in 1941 alone—yet kept fighting. This wasn't because Soviet soldiers loved Stalin (many didn't) but because German occupation was genocidal, because Soviet propaganda was effective, and because the alternative was slavery or death. The Soviet capacity to absorb punishment and continue fighting was perhaps the single most important factor in Allied victory.

**Strategic bombing:** The Combined Bomber Offensive, especially after 1944, devastated German fuel production and transportation. By early 1945, the German army was immobilized by lack of fuel. This was a technological and industrial achievement, not a moral one.

**Codebreaking:** Ultra intelligence gave the Allies insight into German plans and operations. The Battle of the Atlantic turned on breaking Enigma. This was a technical advantage, not a moral superiority.

None of these factors was inevitable. Each represented contingent historical developments—political choices, technological innovations, organizational achievements—that might have gone differently.

## The Uncomfortable Lesson

Here's what I think we get wrong: we want morality and effectiveness to be correlated. We want to believe that good ideas win and bad ideas lose, that history has a moral direction, that the universe punishes evil and rewards virtue.

But morality and effectiveness are orthogonal variables. They can align, but they don't have to. Nazism didn't lose because it was evil. It lost because of contingent factors: Soviet resistance, American industrial capacity, strategic bombing, codebreaking, Hitler's errors. In a counterfactual world where those factors went differently, Nazism could have won—and its evil would have been worse, not better, for its success.

This matters because the assumption that evil is self-defeating leads to complacency. If we believe that terrible ideologies naturally fail, we don't invest in the hard work of opposing them. We don't build the alliances, develop the technologies, make the strategic choices that actually defeat them.

History shows that terrible ideas can be incredibly effective. The conquests of Genghis Khan were morally repugnant and strategically brilliant. The Atlantic slave system was economically productive for centuries. Communist regimes maintained power for generations despite catastrophic human costs. Morality and effectiveness simply aren't the same thing.

## What Should We Conclude?

First, we should be epistemically careful about counterfactual history. We can't run history twice. The claims above are plausible, not certain. German victory in 1940 or 1941 would have created its own problems—governing a continent, managing occupation, potential conflicts with Japan—that might have undone the Reich regardless. Counterfactuals illuminate contingency, but we shouldn't overclaim.

Second, we should reject the comforting narrative that evil contains the seeds of its own destruction. This narrative is false, and believing it makes us less effective at stopping evil when it arises. Bad ideas need to be defeated, not waited out.

Third, we should recognize that the postwar liberal order—NATO, the EU, the United Nations, the web of alliances and institutions—was built by people who understood this. They didn't assume fascism had failed because it was wrong. They assumed it had failed because of specific contingent factors, and they built institutions to ensure those factors remained in place: American commitment to Europe, economic integration, democratic solidarity. The postwar order was insurance against the return of effective evil.

Finally, we should cultivate the ability to evaluate ideas on their actual merits, not their likely success. Nazism was evil not because it failed but because of what it did: the genocide, the aggression, the destruction of human dignity. These moral evaluations stand independent of historical outcomes. A world where Hitler won would not have been a better world. The fact that he lost doesn't make this true—it was true regardless.

The universe doesn't guarantee that good wins. That's on us.
